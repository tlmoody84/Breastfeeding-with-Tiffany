KEY BREASTFEEDING FACTS

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommends that infants be exclusively breastfed for the first 6 months with continued breastfeeding while introducing appropriate complementary foods for 1 year or longer. Key factors. Although most infants receive some breastmilk, most are not exclusively breastfeeding or continuing to breastfeed as long as recommended.

Positioning

There are many different ways to nurse your little one. Make sure to do what is comfortable for you and baby. Just a few to name, are the cross or cradle hold, kaola hold, side laying position etc. Just make sure comfort is top priority.

Working Mom

Alot of people give up on nursing due to working full time. The key to keeping the milk supply while working is to NEVER forget about expressing your milk. Purchasing a breast pump is very beneficial for this purpose. Remember, the more you express, the more your milk will flow. Milk can be stored for future use at the proper recommendation in the freezer or refrigerator.

Benefits

The biggest benefit is protecting you and your baby from different cancers. One of my favorite benefits is the bonding that you will have. My motto is, (if possible), "Why pay for milk when you can get the cow for free?"

How Do You Prepare

Knowledge is Power. Breastfeeding is portrayed in a society as some kind of idyllic, easy and miraculous for babies and moms.

Take a breastfeeding class and have your breast examined.

Create a comfortable place to breastfeed baby. Stock up on breastfeeding essentials. Most importantly, do not be ashamed or afraid to ask for help.

Consistency is key

If we are to support the culture of breastfeeding, consistency is key. We cannot tell a mother that breastfeeding is crucial, but shame her when she does it in public.

Do we expect all breastfeeding mothers to stay at home or remain invisible, simply because some people happen to feel ‘offended’ by the sight of breast?

All mothers – breastfeeding or otherwise – go through long and overwhelming battles. Being patronised about ‘the right way’ to feed their child or ‘how much they can expose while breastfeeding is the last thing they need.

Breastfeeding vs. Bottle Feeding

Breastmilk is a living fluid. It adapts to your baby's changing circumstances. If your baby gets ill, your body makes extra white blood cells and antibobies that travel into your milk to help fight infection. Formulas milk is made of processed skimmed cow's milk with added emulsifiers and stabilizers to help the oils and water mix when you make up the feed.

Discomfort

Discomfort and pain, which are not uncommon, especially during the first two weeks. Some mothers also struggle with painful mastitis or thrush. Although these are disadvantages, none of it is permanent. The pain is only temporary. There are products available for yout comfort.

FACTS for STORAGE

Milk can be stored:

Up to four days in a standard refrigerator.

Up to three to four months inside a standard refrigerator freezer.

Up to six to twelve months in a separate deep freezer.

In an insulated cooler bag with frozen ice packs for up to 24 hours while traveling.

Remember:

It’s normal for stored milk to separate into a cream and milk layer.

It’s also normal for breast milk to appear in a range of colors from slightly yellow to yellow-orange and even green.

The color of your breast milk can change based on what you eat, drink and the medications you take while breastfeeding.

NEVER:

You never want to keep milk at temperatures higher than 77 F (25 C) because of the risk of bacteria growth.

Once your milk has been frozen and thawed, you also never want to refreeze your milk.
